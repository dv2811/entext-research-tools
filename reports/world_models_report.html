1. Executive Summary

AI is undergoing a fundamental shift from language-based models to spatial intelligence. While Large Language Models (LLMs) have demonstrated remarkable capabilities in text generation, they remain "wordsmiths in the dark" — eloquent but ungrounded in physical reality.

Core Insight: Spatial intelligence will transform how we create and interact with real and virtual worlds — revolutionizing storytelling, creativity, robotics, scientific discovery, and beyond.

Leading AI researchers including Fei-Fei Li (Stanford, World Labs), Yann LeCun (Meta), Demis Hassabis (Google DeepMind), and Jim Fan (NVIDIA) are pushing for AIs that learn world models to plan and predict accurately in physical environments.

The Case For World Models, Part I: The Neuroscientific Reason →
Author: Anil Ananthaswamy (Where Machines Think) | Date: February 9, 2026
Core Thesis:

Neuroscientists have known for 150 years that brains use world models. Modern AI is finally catching up to this understanding.

Historical Timeline:
1860s — Hermann von Helmholtz: "Unconscious inference" — brain combines sense data with prior knowledge
1970s — Richard Gregory: Brain as prediction machine (like a scientist hypothesizing and verifying)
1999 — Rao & Ballard: Predictive coding implementation — hierarchical neural network with error correction
2000s — Karl Friston: Active inference — brain forces body to act when models cannot be updated
How Predictive Coding Works:
Sense Data → Layer 1 → Layer 2 → Layer 3 (hypothesis) ↑ ↑ ↑ Errors flow up, predictions flow down
Why LLMs Don't Have True World Models:
  • Not grounded in physical reality
  • Cannot dynamically update after training when predictions are wrong
  • Missing the error-correction mechanism that brains use
Expert Quotes:
Fei-Fei Li "3D world follows laws of physics... To fundamentally extract that information out and be able to represent it and be able to generate it is fundamentally quite a different problem [than language]."
Demis Hassabis "There's still a lot about the spatial dynamics of the world, spatial awareness, the physical context we are in, and how that works mechanically, that is hard to describe in words."
Jim Fan (NVIDIA) World modeling = "predicting the next plausible world state (or a longer duration of states) conditioned on an action."

4. Neuroscience Foundation: Predictive Processing

The brain has been using world models for millions of years. Understanding this evolutionary history provides insight into why modern AI must incorporate similar mechanisms.

Figure: Hierarchical predictive coding - the brain's world model architecture (source: Where Machines Think)

Concept Description AI Implication
Unconscious Inference
Helmholtz, 1860s		 Brain unconsciously infers external causes from sense information by combining actual sensory data with prior knowledge AI must combine learned priors with real-time sensory input
Predictive Processing
Gregory, 1970s		 Brain generates hypotheses about the world and verifies against sensory data, updating models when predictions fail World models need dynamic error correction, not just static training
Predictive Coding
Rao & Ballard, 1999		 Hierarchical neural network where predictions flow down and errors flow up, enabling efficient learning Architecture for world models: hierarchical prediction with error propagation
Active Inference
Friston, 2000s		 When internal models cannot change (e.g., body temperature regulation), brain forces body to act to match predictions Embodied AI must act on environment, not just predict
Key Insight: LLMs lack the error-correction mechanism that allows brains (and future world models) to update their understanding when predictions fail. This is why they hallucinate and cannot learn from experience after training.
